Initiating and securing buy‑in for an off-roadmap payment redesign

Role

Senior Product Designer @ Grab

Status

Launched, Sep 2022

Challenge

Balancing complex business requirements while securing leadership approval for a redesign and articulating how the new experience would drive meaningful business impact toward our goals.

Impact

1 Top-up attempt rates for rides increased by 33.6%, while the food vertical increased by 16.3%.

2 Top-up no longer highlighted as an area of concern in NPS feedback.

01

Background

Why GrabWallet?

When I first joined Grab, I was assigned to the Wallet team. Therefore, I ditched the familiar for the new. I switched up card payments on the Grab app for the GrabWallet.

That’s when I felt the gap in my payment experience. The next time I needed a ride, I went from booking a ride with just one tap to 6-8 taps. Plus having to do quick math.

→ Pre-2022 experience

02

The problems

1

Is there demand for it?

If my hypothesis is correct and topping up before a ride is inconvenient enough to outweigh the benefits of using GrabWallet, users may respond by increasing their top-up amounts or by churning altogether.

2

How can I get it prioritised?

Top-up experience wasn’t on the backlog at that time as it had just been updated a year ago. Assessing whether this friction is significant enough to prioritise it immediately became an important part of the problem.

Following the data

I partnered with my data analyst to gather the available data. Because of data limitations, we could not reliably identify how many transactions failed due to insufficient balance, so we examined related behaviours across other touchpoints.

We found that most users topped up in rounded amounts, often to the nearest multiple of ten, and those adding larger amounts typically did so before entering the booking flow. The average time from top-up to booking was about 24 seconds, leaving users exposed to fare changes.

We also observed that ride-booking users were more likely to switch payment methods than food-ordering users, likely due to the time-sensitive nature of transport.

03

Challenges

Challenge 1: Business appeal

I proposed the idea of enabling users to top up the exact shortfall to my PM and design team, and once aligned, began conversations with product leadership.

However, leadership needed to understand whether the redesign could generate meaningful revenue impact to justify investing in changes to a relatively new top-up flow.

Because GrabWallet payments are up to 5x cheaper to support than credit card transactions, I framed the opportunity from both a cost-efficiency and user retention perspective, partnering with the business team to model the projected savings and potential revenue uplift.

Challenge 2: Design feasibility

We learned that payment networks would not offer preferential rates for top-ups unless the transaction clearly appeared as a top-up.

I addressed this later, after securing internal buy-in, by presenting and negotiating directly with network representatives over several rounds to reach an acceptable compromise.

04

Getting buy-in

Research

My PM and I agreed that we would continue building our case while working on the current projects on our roadmap.

I worked on the prototype, moderated interviews and usability tests, and worked with my analyst to quantify the potential business impact relative to other projects.

Research highlights

One user saw that they had an insufficient balance on the ride screen but did not know how to top up.

I shared the findings with the transport team, but changing the CTA was a no-go. I thus introduced a new top-up entrance within the payment methods screen instead.

Users who preferred to top up just enough are cautious about safety. They also do not want to overspend.

This supported my earlier research about how safety is a concern due to the fraudulent GrabWallet transactions making the news.

Majority of users preferred having the additional confirmation step instead of charging to their card immediately.

This also aligned with earlier research showing that fees on the platform change frequently and users do switch between multiple cards.

Overall, users loved the new flow. Together with the team, we pitched the proposal that finally convinced leadership.

05

Outcomes

Final design

I designed a top up and booking/ordering flow that satisfied all stakeholders’ requirements through frequent reviews with the payment networks, business stakeholders, and the design team.

1 Offered multiple options reminiscent of a top-up screen, with the default being the minimum required amount.

2 Stakeholders wanted to experiment with larger top-up amounts so the additional options made this possible.

3 Preserved minimum top-up amount to avoid losing money on processing fees, which is communicated to users.

Overall impact

+S$950k

Annual savings from reducing user churn to high cost payment methods

+S$480k

Annual revenue from book-through-rate increase

By identifying and championing an enhancement that was not originally on the roadmap, I helped the team deliver a project that ultimately outperformed the other initiatives we completed while trying to get this project prioritised.

It led to a 33.6% increase in top-up attempt rates for rides and a 16.3% increase in the food vertical. This uplift improved book-through-rate which contributed to revenue gains.

The experience also became significantly more efficient for users who prefer to keep a low balance, reducing the flow from eight taps down to two (~24s to 3s). As a result, top-ups stopped appearing as an area of concern in NPS feedback.
