TikTok Case Study (2025)
Jump to...

Optimising code-based payment methods

Role

Senior Product Designer @ TikTok

Status

Launched Oct 2025

Challenge

1 Existing workflow to add new methods was inefficient.

2 Mature payment flow was not a priority to fix despite the inconsistent UI.

Impact

1 Reduced turnaround time when implementing new payment methods by up to a week.

2 Improved the completion rate for new-to-method users by 7.2%.

01

Background

Why code-based methods?

Users purchase Coins to send Gifts on TikTok. In regions with lower card adoption, code-type payment methods like PromptPay and QRIS are low risk and user friendly.

What was happening?

Each time a new payment method was introduced, a designer would be assigned to provide the design spec for the engineering and QA teams to reference during development.

02

Problems & Opportunities

Problem #1

As there were subtle differences between existing code-type payment method UI, it became a time-consuming task to prepare a consistent and complete spec.

Problem #2

I felt that the UI could be improved but it wasn't a priority at that time due to the relatively high Payment Success Rate (PSR).

And herein lies the opportunity...

I wanted to streamline this process to make adding new methods more efficient. However, I needed buy-in from my PM as we worked on strict timelines.

03

Getting buy-in

1 Reduce time spent waiting for approval

To maintain design quality, every change had to be approved at the product level. This meant that even the smallest tasks required Designers to sit for internal review followed by a product review which happen on fixed days of the week. After discussing it with my Design Manager, we agreed that new methods could skip this step if the UI is made more consistent.

2 Templatise the UI

Apart from maintaining design quality, this would also benefit engineers and QA as future implementations will be more predictable. The current implementation of the payment screens also had quite a few legacy bugs that needed to be addressed.

3 Improve on payment instructions

During my audit, I noticed that methods like GoPay did not display payment instructions. Additionally, local Ops feedbacked that the methods with instructions were not well translated.

4 Stick to the original timeline

Most importantly, my PM wanted to be able to launch the new payment method on time. This gave me less than a week in total to deliver so I had to manage my time effectively.

04

Research

Analysing competitors

Familiarity is key as users may already have set expectations about how online payment works. In the interest of time, I focused on gathering references from major region-specific e-commerce and gaming platforms, like Shopee and Codashop. This helped inform Call-to-Action (CTA) choices, such as in cases where there were up to 3 possible CTAs.

Diving into data

Since screenshot activity could not be tracked on the web, my Data Analyst and I extrapolated user behaviour from the click-through-rate on the 'Download QR code' button. Analysis of successful payments showed that 10% of users clicked download while 90% completed without interaction, leading us to conclude that screenshots are the dominant method, though there is still some reliance on downloads. After some consideration, we decided to retain the download call-to-action for less tech-savvy users.

05

Trade-offs

Change vs. consistency

Several tradeoffs were necessary during the design process. The back button had been previously removed to reduce drop-offs. To maintain consistency across the platform, I added the navbar back with the status placed the title.

To help users confirm the selected method before payment and build trust, I also added the method name and logo for familiarity. However, the Content team required the method name to appear in the navbar's title so the positions had to be swapped.

Modules vs. templates

As several methods offered multiple ways to pay, I also explored a module-based approach to clearly demarcate the methods and also allow for greater flexibility for future methods. However, when I reviewed the design internally, I received a question asking if there's two steps to the payment.

As a result, I went with the template approach which trades flexibility for clarity. It also limits the number of potential variations in the layout, making it more consistent for development.

06

Results & Impact

+6.7%

Desktop PSR

More relevant CTAs and instructions led to an increase in Payment Success Rate.

+7.2%

First recharge UV

Users are now more likely to complete their payment when using a code-based payment method for the first time.

Impact on process

This project cut development turnaround time by up to a week and reduced administrative design work. Engineers could start implementing new payment methods without waiting for the final sign-off.

It also revealed the inefficiency in our structured approval process. What was originally meant to safeguard quality had become a bottleneck. Even simple tasks like adding a payment method required formal documentation and full reviews. The administrative overhead felt especially taxing when we were neck-deep in projects.

Additionally, post-development activities like acceptance testing have never been tracked in design time estimates, resulting in bugs and UI issues across all existing payment methods in production. This prompted Design Managers to reassess the process of approvals and estimations for business-as-usual (BAU) work.

Future improvements

Due to the limited timeframe I was working with and the revenue risk associated with any payment-related redesign, I opted for UI that users were more familiar with to play it safe.

With additional time, I would have liked to experiment on changes in a more methodological manner and challenge existing assumptions. For example, running A/B tests to compare the impact of offering one or multiple call-to-actions and also the prominence of the expiry time and other actions.
